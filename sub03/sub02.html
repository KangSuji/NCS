Advanced Placement (AP)
College Board’s Advanced Placement courses and exams in high school are designed to provide you
with an advantage in college by letting you

1. Earn College Credit and Placement
- Your AP score may be transferrable to college credits.
In fact, most AP students who enroll in 4-year colleges start with some credits or prerequisite waivers.

2. Save Money and Time
- Earning credit or placement can open up time on your schedule or even give you an opportunity to graduate early.

3. Stand Out to Colleges
- The letters “AP” on your high school transcript shows colleges that you are motivated to succeed, and taking the exam demonstrates your commitment to tackle and complete college-level work.

4. Keep Your Options Open
- Earning college credit with AP can give you the flexibility to change majors, pursue a second degree or minor, study abroad, or even seek internships.

Honors

Honors courses cover similar material as regular classes but add more depth into the subjects.
Honors courses usually add 0.5 points to your GPA, while AP courses tend to add 1 points to your GPA.

Dual Courses

Dual Credit Courses are year-long, college-level classes taught directly by an affiliated, accredited college.
Upon successful completion of the course, students will receive college credit and grade of the equivalent subject.
Although many colleges/universities accept dual credit courses, students are advised to check with the university they wish to attend.
